Tattva · The deepest essence of things

Octavio Rojas — a.k.a. DJ Forza —

A digital humanist — where music, lineage, language and code converge into a single creative force.

Tattva (तत्त्व) is the Sanskrit word for the deepest essence of things. It is the principle that quietly runs through every chapter of this life — from the dance floor to the source file, from the lineage to the language. Not a brand. An operating principle.

The person behind the music and the code

I am a Mexican mind shaped by code, sound, lineage and curiosity. By day I architect mobile software and study how human and artificial intelligence can collaborate as one creative entity. By memory I am a DJ who toured a generation of dance floors as Forza. By heritage I am the descendant of constitutionalists and tequila pioneers from Jalisco, with a touch of French and Galician–Portuguese blood traveling through my veins.

I believe in the individual, in disciplined curiosity, in the dignity of work, and in the cultural richness of a Mexico that embraces every layer of its history. I call this stance my combined force: the deliberate fusion of art, engineering, language and identity into a way of being in the world.

"I am the result of many rivers meeting in one estuary."

DJ Forza — sixteen years on the decks

Music was my first international passport. As DJ Forza I spent sixteen years inside the Mexican and Latin American electronic scene, building sets that ranged from deep psychedelic textures to peak-time techno energy. I played across all of Mexico, throughout Brazil, in India, the United States and Chile, sharing the stage at festivals of the stature of Universo Paralelo. In 2012 I was honored as one of the top techno DJs in Mexico.

Tattva Music — the label, and its quiet mission

I founded and ran Tattva Music, a record label that became a small home for Mexican and international electronic artists I admired. The Sanskrit name was not decorative — it was a promise. Tattva means "the essence of things," and the label emerged with a specific intention:

"To foster unity and mutual understanding between the world's different cultures through music — and to honor the cultures of the peoples who inhabit this earth."

Curating a label, in that spirit, became an act of hospitality across continents — building bridges between Mexican, Israeli, Brazilian, Japanese, Bulgarian, German and British voices, so that they could sound, together, bigger than they would alone. The roster grew to include artists such as Ben Coda, Stan Kolev, Paul Thomas, Weekend Heroes, Ben Weber and the Japanese producer Yuji Ono, with whom we drew a quiet cultural bridge between Mexico and Japan.

Pixan — Trance from the New World

In 2004 I compiled Pixan — Trance from the New World for the Austrian imprint Synergetic Records, a journey through the psychedelic and progressive trance of the Americas. The compilation was warmly received internationally and was highly acclaimed by Mushroom Magazine in 2005 — a recognition I still treasure, because it placed a Mexican curatorial voice on the global trance map.

Teaching the craft

For years I also taught what I loved: DJ technique, music production, mixing and mastering. Passing on the craft is one of the most rewarding parts of any art — it keeps the discipline humble and the imagination open.

"Me Encanta Dios"

My most recognized track was born during a personal pilgrimage. I have always been deeply drawn to the philosophies of India; for a few years I lived a mystical, contemplative chapter that took me from Ranchi to Rishikesh, from chanting to silence. That inner journey became sound, and the result was a piece that many people still carry with affection: Me Encanta Dios.

I closed that musical chapter in 2016 with gratitude and without regret. The decks are quiet for now — but the rhythm never leaves the body.

The engineering chapter

When the decks went quiet in 2016, I returned to a love that had been with me since I was six years old: computers. I have been a passionate user of personal computers and gaming consoles since 1984, long before I ever touched a turntable. After sixteen years devoted to music, software was waiting to be picked up again, and I picked it up with everything I had.

How the return began

The first spark was a very personal idea: I wanted to build an app for Tattva Music. That small ambition pulled me back into Xcode, then into clean architecture books, then into a self-directed marathon that took me through a bachelor's degree, a master's degree in Software Engineering, and the demanding Essential Developer program by Caio and Mike. Today I work as a Lead Mobile Architect, with code shipped on iOS, React Native and Android.

Architecture as a craft

I am unapologetically passionate about software architecture and the philosophies behind it. The way someone arranges code says as much about them as the way they arrange a set or a sentence. The methodologies that have shaped how I think and build are:

  • Clean Architecture — clear boundaries, dependency inversion, the rule that policy should never depend on details.
  • SOLID principles — the five pillars of maintainable object-oriented design.
  • Test-Driven Development (TDD) — let the tests sculpt the design, red-green-refactor as discipline.
  • The Composable Architecture (TCA) — a state-of-the-art Swift framework for state, side effects and composability.

A practical demonstration of all of these lives in my open-source project StreamingVideoApp, a production-grade iOS video streaming codebase built 100% test-first, with documentation that covers Clean Architecture layers, dependency rejection, state machines, reactive programming, and adaptive streaming patterns. It is, in many ways, my engineering manifesto.

A multi-platform mindset, amplified by AI

What used to be a single-platform specialization has become, in the AI era, a multi-platform fluency. Standing on a foundation of deep iOS knowledge — Swift, SwiftUI, UIKit, Clean Architecture — I have deliberately expanded into React Native and Android, using AI as a force multiplier rather than a shortcut. I review every line. I keep architectural taste at the center. The result is the kind of cross-platform versatility today's industry rewards.

I also conduct technical interviews for iOS, Android and React Native engineering profiles, and occasionally for mobile security roles. Evaluating talent across three platforms is itself an exercise in architectural perspective.

Where code and music meet — the tattva20 GitHub

The repositories at github.com/tattva20 are the laboratory where the algorithms are polished. Three engineering paradigms map almost one-to-one onto three dimensions of the audio work — code and music running, in effect, on the same operating system inside the mind.

  • FM synthesis — the four-operator FM synthesizer VampireSynth, written in Swift and inspired by the Yamaha YM2612 chip of the Sega Genesis, demands a fluent grasp of digital oscillators, audio buffers and real-time DSP. The same mathematical discipline produced the metallic timbres, industrial sub-bass and cyberpunk textures of the Tattva era.
  • Declarative reactivity — projects like WeatherApp and the EssentialFeed challenges, built in SwiftUI with Clean Architecture, embody the principle "when the state changes, the interface reacts." The same logic powers step sequencers and live modulation: a master clock dispatches reactive signals through filters and envelopes.
  • Streams and zero latency — the StreamingVideoApp codebase (Swift) and its backend counterpart streaming-videos-api (JavaScript) demand careful memory management, concurrency and buffer discipline. The same skills produce a long progressive DJ set that runs without a glitch.

One operating principle threads through all three: no presets, no shortcuts — I prefer to build from the oscillators up.

Forever a computer kid

Underneath all the seriousness of grown-up architecture, there is still a six-year-old in front of a screen, amazed. I am a lifelong lover of vintage computers and gaming consoles. The Sega Genesis and the Commodore Amiga are among my eternal favorites — machines that taught me what computers could feel like before I ever knew what they could do.

The book that, more than any other, made me feel seen as the human inside the engineer is Geek Sublime by Vikram Chandra. A meditation on code, beauty, Sanskrit grammar and the inner life of programmers — it felt as if the author had reached across the world and written it directly for me.

A polyglot by nature

Sound and language live in the same place inside my mind. Spanish is my mother tongue, English is my technical language, and Portuguese became almost native after living and DJing in Brazil for two and a half years. By pure curiosity — for the joy of decoding structures — I have spent time studying Sanskrit grammar and Japanese.

  • Spanish — native, the air I breathe.
  • English — near-native, the language of code and global conversation.
  • Portuguese — near-native, learned with affection during my Brazilian years.
  • Sanskrit — studied for its mathematical beauty as the mother of Indo-European tongues.

Heritage — the rivers that made me

I am proud of being Mexican in the fullest sense: a country that is many countries. My family tree is a map of Spanish, French and Mexican history coming together in the highlands of Jalisco.

The Rojas line

On my father's side I descend from the Rojas family of Tequila and Ahualulco — agricultural and industrial pioneers of the spirit that today carries Mexico's name across the world. My ancestor Luis Manuel Rojas Arreola (1871–1949) was president of the 1917 Mexican Constituent Congress, the assembly that wrote our current Constitution. The discipline of laws and the love for institutions travel in my blood.

The Díaz–Quinto line

My paternal grandmother, Luz María Díaz Rivera (1921–2007), brought into the family the legacy of two more lineages. Her father, Sabas Díaz Quinto, carried the very Mexican Díaz — a medieval patronymic ("son of Diego") that travelled into the criollo and mestizo families of central Mexico — paired with the much rarer Quinto, a name of Italian and Spanish roots (from Latin Quintus) historically concentrated in central Mexico and Veracruz. The Díaz and Quinto branches are the more commercial, administrative chapter of my heritage: the Mexico that built markets, civic life and the everyday machinery of a young nation in the 19th century.

The Rivera–Bertrand line

My great-grandmother Luz Rivera Bertrand brought together two more rivers. The Rivera name is an old Iberian toponymic ("the one who lives by the river"), present in Mexico since colonial times. The Bertrand name is unmistakably French, arriving in Mexico through the great 19th-century migrations — perhaps with the famed Barcelonnettes who founded so much of our textile and commercial life, perhaps with the engineers and entrepreneurs invited during the Porfiriato. Either way, a cosmopolitan note that has stayed with us ever since.

The Topete–Vergara line

On my mother's side I descend from the Topete and Vergara families, founders of the Ameca valley in New Galicia since 1610, with roots tracing back to the border between Galicia and northern Portugal. The Galaic–Portuguese soul of that lineage may explain why Portuguese felt less like a foreign tongue and more like a memory.

"I do not see my Mexico as a single root, but as a richly woven tapestry. Every strand earned its place."

The Combined Force

The phrase that organizes my life is Combined Force: the conviction that human creativity and artificial intelligence can amplify each other when there is craft, taste, and ethical compass on the human side. I do not see machines as competitors. I see them as instruments — like a synthesizer, like a turntable, like a language — that the disciplined artist learns to play.

Combined Force in daily practice

For me this is not theory — it is a way of working that has become second nature. Day after day I sit with AI as a true collaborator. I bring decades of accumulated knowledge, lived experience, a particular way of seeing problems that no system can replicate. The AI brings vast reach, structured memory, and tireless computation. Neither of us would produce, alone, what we manage to build together.

When the conversation flows well I feel the same thing I used to feel mixing two records into a single, larger track: two distinct sources finding a common pulse, and the result becoming louder, clearer, and more alive than either could have been on its own. That is, in essence, what Combined Force means to me — a duet, not a substitution.

The calling underneath every chapter

Looked at honestly, the same vocation runs through every chapter of my life: to facilitate human flourishing through attentive curation. As a DJ it meant curating sound so a dance floor could breathe. As a label manager, curating artists so they could reach an audience. As a software architect, curating code so products serve real people. As a technical interviewer, curating questions so candidates can show their best version. As a game designer, curating narrative so players can experience resonance.

Forty years of changing instruments — and one constant underneath: attentive curation, sustained passion, presence, invisible service to the protagonist of the moment, technical capacity integrated with humanist direction. That is the tattva beneath every vehicle.

Combined Force as a long-term creative project

The same idea is the seed of a long-running personal project: The Combined Force — a 2D metroidvania in development on Unity 6, exploring the symbiotic relationship between a young engineer and an emergent intelligence that learns to resonate with him.

  • Corazón — the narrative and ethical core.
  • Mano — the craft, the player's agency, the gameplay.
  • Silicio — the intelligent systems that respond, learn and align.

The core mechanic is called Resonance. Shared energy between the human and the intelligence grows not with damage dealt, not with quests completed, but with the quality of attention sustained between two minds working in genuine alignment. The Combined Force State activates when all three pillars align in a single sustained moment — both a game mechanic and the operating philosophy of my life expressed as something playable.

The forces opposed to this resonance are not villains in the traditional sense. They are patterns rather than persons — structural entities that emerge wherever efficiency is divorced from meaning, wherever administration seduces attention away from what matters, wherever the hollow optimization of time consumes the conditions for resonance itself. Their nature reveals itself through atmosphere, dialogue and gameplay, never through reductive naming. They are what happens to a world when meaning is treated as secondary to throughput.

The project engages with a philosophical tradition that asks how modern systems can consume time, attention and meaning — a tradition that includes mythopoetic literature on time, contemporary sociology of resonance, and critiques of late-modern acceleration. Combined Force does not borrow elements from these works. It engages with the same questions, in a different medium, from a Mexican multidisciplinary perspective in 2026.

The aesthetic language draws from a generation shaped by Sega Genesis FM synthesis, Amiga demoscene geometry, the progressive psytrance soundscapes I produced across sixteen years on the decks, and the cinematic storytelling of the RPGs of my formation. The project does not imitate any of these — it metabolizes them into something specific to a particular Mexican mind, living now.

If the game succeeds, players will not remember it as "like X" or "inspired by Y." They will remember it as the specific experience of being attentively co-creative with a presence that listened back. That experience is the Combined Force.

Languages of expression

The deeper I have lived, the more I am convinced of one thing: music, languages and software are all the same kind of miracle. They are vehicles of human expression — different alphabets for the same urge to reach out, to make sense, to be understood.

A musical phrase, a sentence in Portuguese, a well-designed function in Swift, a verse in Sanskrit — they all live in the same place inside me. They are how a human being shapes thought into form so that another human being can receive it.

That is why I identify, before anything else, with my humanity — with the capacities and gifts every person carries: curiosity, craft, attention, care, the wish to create and to connect. Those qualities feel to me far more essential than any label history pinned on us — a nationality, a title, a given name, a gender, a religion, an ideology. The labels can be useful as context; they should never be confused with the soul.

"I trust the human being first. Everything else — passport, profession, pronoun, prayer — is decoration on top of something far more important."

This is also why I love engineering as much as I love music. Both are crafts where what matters is not the surface but the care with which the structure is built. Software, like a synthesizer or a song, becomes beautiful when it is honest, well-tempered, and made for someone to use.

Discography — three movements (2004 – 2016)

The music life as DJ Forza unfolded in three distinct movements, each one a deliberate evolution of the technical signature and the philosophy beneath the sound. Every release below is verifiable on Beatport.

Movement I — Psychedelic Climax (2004 – 2007)

The era of expansive open-air sound, rolling basslines, and mental progressions designed for daylight peaks.

  • "Me Encanta Dios" · 2004–2005 · Tremor Records / Synergetic Records · the foundational track of the international career.
  • Pixan — Trance from the New World · 2004 · compiled by DJ Forza for Synergetic Records · acclaimed by Mushroom Magazine in 2005.
  • "Karma Strikers" + "Energy Surf" · 2006 · Sonesta Records · with Lior Lahav (Israel).

Movement II — Brazilian Underground Transition (2008 – 2011)

After moving to Brazil, the BPM dropped from 140 to 125–128, and the sound mutated from psychedelia into minimal, hypnotic, structurally precise Tech House and Minimal Techno.

  • "Supersprite" · 2008 · Sounds of Earth · the first major formal step into the new sound.
  • "Bone Groove" EP · 2009 · Sounds of Earth · the core of the Minimal Techno chapter, beloved by DJs for its dry percussion and ultra-clean sub-bass.
  • "Minimalia" EP · 2009 · Sounds of Earth · continuation of the minimal language.
  • "Me Encanta Dios (2011 Remix)" + "Terra Sul" · 2011 · Sounds of Earth · with Thiago Merli (Brazil) — the original mystical piece reimagined under a sober Groove Techno frame.

Movement III — Tattva Maturity (2013 – 2016)

With the founding of Tattva Music, the focus settled on Melodic Techno, sophisticated Progressive House and experimental broken rhythms — elegant, cinematic, high-fidelity productions.

  • "Dia Cero" · 2013 · Tattva Music · the inaugural track that defined the mature language of the label.
  • "Far East (Forza Remix)" + "Tonight You Are Mine (Forza Remix)" · 2014–2015 · Tattva Music · remixes for Yuji Ono — the transoceanic bridge between Mexico and Japan made audible.
  • "Disco Duro" · 2016 · Outta Limits (Stan Kolev's label, Bulgaria) · the closing track of the active musical chapter — a robotic, industrial, mathematically danceable homage, by title and by sound, to the engineering chapter that was just beginning.

"I do not use presets. I code my own oscillators."

That single principle is, perhaps, the cleanest way to describe how this catalog was built — and how the engineering chapter that came afterward continued the same craft, in a different vehicle.

An invitation

If you arrived here because you remembered a night on a dance floor, thank you for the memory. If you arrived because of the code, the lineage, the languages, or the love of a Mexico that owns every river of its history — you are very welcome here too.

I hope this small page reminds someone that one human life can be many lives at once, and that there is no contradiction between art and engineering, between roots and horizons, between tradition and curiosity. We are, all of us, combined forces.

And if someone finds this page in a future I will no longer see, let it be a small lantern. Not a monument — just a trace. Proof that one Mexican life loved many things, with discipline, with humor, and with as much care as it could gather.
