The engineering chapter

Software architecture as craft. From the Yamaha YM2612 chip of my childhood Genesis to production iOS systems shipping today. One operating principle: build from the oscillators up.

Software, returning

When the decks went quiet in 2016, I returned to a love that had been with me since I was six years old: computers. I have been a passionate user of personal computers and gaming consoles since 1984, long before I ever touched a turntable. After sixteen years devoted to music, software was waiting to be picked up again, and I picked it up with everything I had.

How the return began

The first spark was a very personal idea: I wanted to build an app for Tattva Music. That small ambition pulled me back into Xcode, then into clean architecture books, then into a self-directed marathon that took me through a bachelor's degree, a master's degree in Software Engineering, and the demanding Essential Developer program by Caio and Mike. Today I work as a Lead Mobile Architect, with code shipped on iOS, React Native and Android.

Architecture as a craft

I am unapologetically passionate about software architecture and the philosophies behind it. The way someone arranges code says as much about them as the way they arrange a set or a sentence. The methodologies that have shaped how I think and build are:

  • Clean Architecture — clear boundaries, dependency inversion, the rule that policy should never depend on details.
  • SOLID principles — the five pillars of maintainable object-oriented design.
  • Test-Driven Development (TDD) — let the tests sculpt the design, red-green-refactor as discipline.
  • The Composable Architecture (TCA) — a state-of-the-art Swift framework for state, side effects and composability.

A practical demonstration of all of these lives in my open-source project StreamingVideoApp, a production-grade iOS video streaming codebase built 100% test-first, with documentation that covers Clean Architecture layers, dependency rejection, state machines, reactive programming, and adaptive streaming patterns. It is, in many ways, my engineering manifesto.

A multi-platform mindset, amplified by AI

What used to be a single-platform specialization has become, in the AI era, a multi-platform fluency. Standing on a foundation of deep iOS knowledge — Swift, SwiftUI, UIKit, Clean Architecture — I have deliberately expanded into React Native and Android, using AI as a force multiplier rather than a shortcut. I review every line. I keep architectural taste at the center. The result is the kind of cross-platform versatility today's industry rewards.

I also conduct technical interviews for iOS, Android and React Native engineering profiles, and occasionally for mobile security roles. Evaluating talent across three platforms is itself an exercise in architectural perspective.

Where code and music meet — the tattva20 GitHub

The repositories at github.com/tattva20 are the laboratory where the algorithms are polished. Three engineering paradigms map almost one-to-one onto three dimensions of the audio work — code and music running, in effect, on the same operating system inside the mind.

  • FM synthesis — the four-operator FM synthesizer VampireSynth, written in Swift and inspired by the Yamaha YM2612 chip of the Sega Genesis, demands a fluent grasp of digital oscillators, audio buffers and real-time DSP. The same mathematical discipline produced the metallic timbres, industrial sub-bass and cyberpunk textures of the Tattva era.
  • Declarative reactivity — projects like WeatherApp and the EssentialFeed challenges, built in SwiftUI with Clean Architecture, embody the principle "when the state changes, the interface reacts." The same logic powers step sequencers and live modulation: a master clock dispatches reactive signals through filters and envelopes.
  • Streams and zero latency — the StreamingVideoApp codebase (Swift) and its backend counterpart streaming-videos-api (JavaScript) demand careful memory management, concurrency and buffer discipline. The same skills produce a long progressive DJ set that runs without a glitch.

One operating principle threads through all three: no presets, no shortcuts — I prefer to build from the oscillators up.

Forever a computer kid

Underneath all the seriousness of grown-up architecture, there is still a six-year-old in front of a screen, amazed. I am a lifelong lover of vintage computers and gaming consoles. The Sega Genesis and the Commodore Amiga are among my eternal favorites — machines that taught me what computers could feel like before I ever knew what they could do.

The book that, more than any other, made me feel seen as the human inside the engineer is Geek Sublime by Vikram Chandra. A meditation on code, beauty, Sanskrit grammar and the inner life of programmers — it felt as if the author had reached across the world and written it directly for me.
